# YouTube Script — "The Ground Is Shaking. I Started Building."

**Date:** March 8, 2026
**Author:** Marketing Director + Copywriter
**Length:** 6-8 minutes
**Format:** Founder on camera. Conversational. One take energy. Can cut between angles but no b-roll required.
**Arc:** Underdog > Wonder > Vision (Personal story > Observations > What's next)
**Thumbnail:** "The volcano is smoking. Nobody's looking up."

---

## The Script

I've been in the corporate world for over 15 years. Marketing executive. Led teams, managed budgets, navigated the whole corporate landscape. And when AI came out, I was all in. Day one. Heavy user. I was the guy in the office showing people what ChatGPT could do, getting my team to try new tools, pushing adoption across the org.

But I never saw it as a threat. I saw it as a really good assistant. A faster way to do things we were already doing. And honestly, I think most people still see it that way.

And then about a month ago, something shifted.

You ever watch a movie where someone's at a resort, or a town near a volcano, and they see smoke? Everyone else is having a great time. Taking photos. Going about their day. But this one person feels the ground rumble. And they get this look on their face. Because they know. Something big is about to happen.

That's the moment I had. Not because I read an article or saw a keynote. Because I started building.

I went from using AI to building with AI. And the gap between those two things is massive. Using AI is asking it questions. Building with AI is giving it a task and watching it execute. Writing code, reviewing code, shipping features. Not talking about work. Doing work.

And what I realized is that what one person can do right now, today, with the right setup, would have required a team of 10 people a year ago. I'm not exaggerating. That's my actual lived experience over the past 30 days.

So that's the volcano. Now let me tell you what I noticed on the way up.

---

Every AI tool you've ever used starts the same way. A blank textbox. ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Copilot. All of them. Open the app, and there it is. A cursor blinking at you. And the promise is: it can do anything.

But if you've spent any time in product or marketing or design, you know that "it can do anything" is actually a UX problem. People don't do well with an everything button. We've known this for decades.

Think about video games. Some players love open world. Skyrim, GTA, Zelda. Give them a map and they'll spend 400 hours finding every secret, breaking every system, doing things the developers never imagined. And that's what's happening right now with tools like Claude Code. People are using a coding tool to write pitch decks and legal documents and business plans. These open world thinkers took a blank canvas and built things nobody expected. They pushed AI further than anyone planned.

But most people aren't open world players. Most people are story mode. Side-scrollers. They want structure. They want a guided experience. They want to open an app and know what to do next.

And right now, nobody is building that. The entire AI industry is racing to build more powerful blank textboxes. More capable open worlds. But who's building the guided game? Who's making AI accessible for the people who don't want to engineer prompts from scratch every single time?

That question stuck with me. And it became the second thing that changed how I think about all of this.

---

Because once you see the gap, you start seeing it everywhere.

I work with enterprise software every day. Tools like HubSpot, Salesforce, you name it. And these companies got huge by solving real problems. HubSpot tied marketing to a CRM and made it trackable. That was brilliant. But then they expanded. They bolted on more tools. And more. And more. Until you're paying thousands a month for a platform your team uses maybe 15 to 20 percent of.

It's the multi-tool problem. They sold you on the Swiss Army knife that can do everything. But the scissors are bad. The saw is bad. The only thing that actually works is the blade. And you're still paying for all of it.

So once I started building with AI, the very first question I asked wasn't "what's cool?" It was "what can we stop paying for?"

And the answer was... a lot.

Because the thing about bloated enterprise software is that most of what it does is simple. It's forms. It's dashboards. It's data moving from one place to another. And when you have AI that can actually build things, not just talk about them, you can create a custom solution in days that does exactly what your team needs. Nothing more, nothing less. Purpose-built. Clean. Focused.

That's already happening. Early adopters are trimming the fat. They're building internal tools with AI that replace platforms they've been overpaying for. And it's giving them an edge that compounds over time. Because every month, they're getting better at building, and the bloated platforms are getting more bloated.

The people who learn to build with AI right now aren't going to be replaced by it. They're going to be the most valuable people in their companies. The ones who can look at a problem and say, "I can build a solution for that by Friday."

---

So. Where does all of this go?

This is the question I can't stop thinking about. Because if AI is moving toward action, not just advice, then a lot of what we call "software" starts to look... unnecessary.

Think about it. You ask Copilot to save a file and it tells you how to save a file. That's advice. Meanwhile, the tools that are actually winning right now are the ones that do the thing. Not explain the thing.

So if AI can open a browser and shop for you, what's a mouse for? If AI can find any file with a single prompt, why are we still clicking through folder hierarchies? If AI can write the email, schedule the meeting, summarize the notes, and update the project board, what are we doing with four separate apps for that?

We're not losing tools. We're outgrowing them. And I think what comes next is so much more interesting than what we have now.

Instead of 80 apps on your phone that you use 9 of, imagine one intelligent layer that understands what you want and coordinates everything. Not an app. An orchestration layer. Something that knows your context, remembers your preferences, and actually acts on your behalf.

That's not science fiction. I've been building it.

---

The project is called Flow by Kaivoo. And the reason I'm telling you all of this, the volcano, the blank textbox, the bloat, the future, is because Flow is my answer to all of it.

It's an AI agent platform. Not a chatbot. Not another blank textbox. A team of AI agents with real roles, real memory, and real safety rails. You can use any AI model, including free local ones that run on your machine. Buy once, own it forever. No subscriptions.

And the part that still blows my mind: the app was built by its own agent system. 33 sprints. 12 AI agents. The entire git history proves it. The system that built the product is the product.

I'm not saying this to sell you something today. We launch April 14th. But I wanted to share the thinking behind it. Because I think we're at one of those rare moments where the ground is shifting and the people who notice have a real chance to build something meaningful.

The volcano is smoking. Most people haven't looked up yet.

I looked up. And I started building.

My name is Ricky. If any of this resonated, subscribe. I'm going to be sharing this entire journey, the building, the thinking, the mistakes, all of it. And if you want to check out what I'm making, the link is in the description.

Thanks for watching. I'll see you in the next one.

## Post-Production Notes

- **No script teleprompter.** Internalize the beats and talk naturally. The exact words don't matter as much as the energy and the ideas.
- **Four acts:** The Volcano (personal story), The Blank Textbox (industry observation), Trim the Fat (practical insight), Where Does Software Go (vision). Each act is roughly 90 seconds.
- **Transitions between acts are conversational.** "So that's the volcano. Now let me tell you what I noticed." "Because once you see the gap, you start seeing it everywhere." "So. Where does all of this go?"
- **Flow mention comes at the end, naturally.** 5+ minutes of pure thought leadership before you name the product. The audience trusts you by then.
- **Energy should build.** Start reflective (volcano story), get observational (blank textbox), get practical (trim the fat), end visionary and slightly fired up (where does software go + Flow reveal).
- **Thumbnail options:** "The volcano is smoking. Nobody's looking up." or "I went from AI skeptic to builder in 30 days."
